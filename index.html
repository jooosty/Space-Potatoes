What is NEBULA Xplorer?

NEBULA – Xplorer stands for Netherlands Educational Satellite for Exploration of Binary-Linked Astrophysics – X-ray Observer. Approximately four hundred students help SRON, fourteen Dutch educational institutions, and many industrial partners to develop this space mission from start to finish — guided by scientists and engineers. SRON leads the mission, shaping a new generation of scientists, designers, and engineers for Dutch space research.

Scientific Goal

Better understand how a black hole strips material from an orbiting companion star and how the resulting jets of energy are formed.

Educational Goal

Give students from Dutch educational institutions a first-hand experience of building a space mission from the ground up.

01

X-ray Binary Mysteries

Many of the brightest objects in the universe are X-ray binaries — combinations of an extremely compact object, such as a black hole or neutron star, and a companion star. Over time, the compact object strips matter from its companion, releasing enormous energy in a focused beam called a jet. Scientists do not yet fully understand this process, and the behaviour of matter right at the edge of the black hole remains one of the great mysteries of modern astrophysics.

These behaviours vary across a huge range of timescales — from milliseconds to months. A platform fully under SRON's control allows continuous, uninterrupted observations that existing major observatories simply cannot provide.

Known targets include Cygnus X-1 and IGR J17091−3624 — objects exhibiting unique X-ray variability that are key to advancing our understanding of accretion processes.

02

Long-Duration Observations

NEBULA – Xplorer will study how jets form and how X-ray binaries evolve by observing these objects over extended periods — tracking how their emissions vary across timescales ranging from milliseconds to weeks. These long observation windows make it possible to combine X-ray data with measurements from other telescopes across different wavelengths.

Crucially, black hole behaviour in stellar-mass X-ray binaries has a proven scaling relation with supermassive black holes in active galactic nuclei (AGN). By studying nearby stellar-mass black holes — observable on human timescales — we gain direct insight into galactic-scale processes that would otherwise take millions of years to unfold.

03

Training the Next Generation

Every six months, a new cohort of students from different disciplines — including astrophysics, spacecraft engineering, optics, electronics, software, and communications — joins the project. Each group builds on the work of the last, with continuity ensured by SRON scientists and industry partners.
