Optics System

Wolter Type 1
Mirror Modules

NEBULA-Xplorer focuses X-rays using double-reflecting Wolter type 1 mirror modules — 30 thin-foil gold-coated concentric conical mirrors per concentrator, each just 20 cm long and 10 cm in diameter. Gold coatings maximise X-ray reflectivity at the shallow grazing-incidence angles required.

The compact design results in an 85 cm focal length, directing photons onto individual Silicon Drift Detectors housed in the Focal Plane Assembly. Dedicated alignment modules ensure precise concentration of photons onto each SDD.

Detection System

Silicon Drift Detectors

Each concentrator focuses X-ray photons onto a dedicated Silicon Drift Detector (SDD) — commercial off-the-shelf devices selected for reliability and performance. The flight model carries 12 SDDs in total, with 2 reserved for in-flight calibration using a small radioactive source.

To reach optimal sensitivity, all detectors are cooled to −35°C via thermal strapping to a dedicated radiator on the satellite exterior. Straight-light baffles (black cones) protect each SDD from stray photons. The signal from every SDD is processed by hot-redundant Instrument Readout Units with space-grade digital signal processing.

The IRUs record X-ray energies with precise timing information — enabling the spectral-timing science that defines the NEBULA mission.

Electronics

Readout
Electronics

Two hot-redundant Instrument Readout Units (IRUs) convert photon detection pulses from the 12 Silicon Drift Detectors into calibrated energy and precise UTC timestamps, then relay science data to the spacecraft C&DH system.

Signal Chain
Detector (×12 SDD)
Amptek FastSDD — X-ray photon absorbed → voltage pulse, height ∝ photon energy (3.6 mV/keV). Rise time ~65 ns.
Pre-amplifier
Amptek PA-210 charge-sensitive amplifier. Integrates detector current pulse into voltage step. 130V bias via Cockroft-Walton generator.
ADC
TI ADC3683-SP — 18-bit, up to 65 MSPS, space-qualified. LVDS output interface. COTS variant (ADC3683) used for EM/prototyping.
FPGA — Digital Pulse Processor
Microchip RT PolarFire RTPF5000 — radiation-hardened. Replaces legacy analogue shaping filters. Extracts photon energy & timing at full flexibility.
MCU
Microchip SAM3X8ERT — in-flight FPGA reprogramming capability. Novel feature enabling firmware updates after launch.
Local Oscillator + GPS Timestamp
Microchip 2105 series oscillator (1 ppm stability). GPS absolute timestamp anchors the local clock — 1 µs timing resolution maintained without drift.
Output → C&DH
RS485 redundant bus. Each photon packet: absolute time (s) + photon energy (eV) + timestamp (µs). Max data rate: 76.4 kbps. CCSDS-based packet format.

Operations

System
Modes

Six operational modes govern the spacecraft's behaviour throughout the mission. The spacecraft transitions between modes autonomously or by ground command.

Mode 01
Launch
Launch Mode
From launch to spacecraft separation. All on-board equipment is off except essential systems. Battery fully charged before launch. No solar power generated.
Mode 02
Acquisition
Sun Acquisition Mode
From separation until nominal Sun-pointing attitude is achieved. No energy from solar arrays yet — batteries supply power. Only essential functions active: thermal management, AOCS, and communications.
Mode 03
Safe Mode
Safe Mode
Used for failure recovery. Only units essential to the spacecraft's survival conditions are active: Sun pointing, thermal control, power management. Communications enabled to facilitate return to nominal mode.
Mode 04
Nominal
Stand-by Mode
Applicable when not thrusting nor acquiring science data. All on-board equipment is on except the instrument, which is in stand-by. Also used for communications and housekeeping operations.
Mode 05
Science
Science Mode
S/C configuration where scientific observations are being made by the X-ray instrument. All other units are on. IRUs recording photon energy and timestamps at full data rate. Primary operational mode.
Mode 06
Manoeuvre
Orbit Control Mode
Applicable when performing orbital manoeuvres using the Dawn 4U Cubedrive propulsion module. All on-board equipment is on except the instrument, which is in stand-by.
Instrument Structure

Optical Bench
Assembly

The OBA houses all 10 concentrators in a 3-2-3-2 hexagonal layout machined from 30 mm thick Al 6061-T6. Three titanium bipod mounts form a quasi-kinematic isostatic interface to the spacecraft, allowing free thermal expansion without transferring stress.

Alignment is achieved by shimming each concentrator's tilt angle using a UV collimated beam to sub-20 µm precision. The FPA connects via six aluminium side panels with triangular cutouts for efficient load transfer and X-ray pass-through holes.

Science Case

What NEBULA
Observes

NEBULA-Xplorer targets X-ray binaries — systems where a black hole or neutron star devours material from a companion star, generating bright, variable X-ray emission. Continuous long-term observations impossible on existing facilities become routine for NEBULA.

Cygnus X-1
High-mass black hole X-ray binary
One of the brightest persistent X-ray sources in the sky. Exhibits unique spectral-timing variability on timescales from milliseconds to months — ideal for NEBULA's long-cadence, continuous observations.
IGR J17091−3624
Black hole X-ray binary — transient
Shows "heartbeat" quasi-periodic oscillations (QPOs) that are not fully understood. Regular high-cadence monitoring by NEBULA will constrain how these QPO behaviours evolve physically and temporally.
Active Galactic Nuclei
Supermassive black holes — scaling relations
Black hole processes have a mass-scaling relation: behaviour in stellar-mass black holes replicates on non-human timescales in AGN. Understanding stellar-mass binaries directly deepens our knowledge of galaxy evolution.
Neutron Star Binaries
Compact object — accretion timing
X-ray binaries containing neutron stars are key laboratories for accretion physics. NEBULA's 200 cm² effective area at 1 keV enables high-count-rate timing analysis, advancing our understanding of dense matter physics.
